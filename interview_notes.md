# Interview Notes: GDPR Compliance Gap Analysis

## Short Project Explanation

This project looks at how a small recruitment agency could improve the way it handles GDPR requests and personal data.

I used Greenline Recruitment, a fictional Dublin-based agency, because recruitment businesses handle a lot of personal data but often rely on email, Excel, and shared folders.

The main issue was that the team could probably handle requests day to day, but they could not easily prove what happened. That was the real risk.

## How I Would Explain It in an Interview

I looked at a small recruitment agency that stores candidate CVs, ID documents, interview notes, client contacts, and some payroll details for temporary staff.

The company did not have a formal GDPR system. Requests were coming in by email, and someone would usually deal with them manually.

The problem was not that the team ignored GDPR. It was that the process relied too much on memory and individual follow-up.

I mapped the current process, identified the main gaps, and suggested a more controlled but still realistic process using a simple tracker and data register.

I kept the recommendations lightweight because a big compliance platform would probably be too much for an 18-person company.

## Key Talking Points

- I chose recruitment because it is a good example of a small business handling sensitive personal data every day.
- The biggest issue was lack of visibility. They did not have one place to see what data existed or what requests were open.
- Consent was being collected informally, but there was no strong evidence trail.
- My recommendation was practical: use a simple tracker, assign ownership, record due dates, and keep closure evidence.
- The value was reduced risk, less manual searching, and more confidence when responding to candidates.

## Questions I Might Be Asked

### Why did you choose GDPR?

Because it is a real business issue, especially for companies that handle personal data but do not have mature systems. It also shows process thinking, not just documentation.

### What was the biggest gap?

The biggest gap was the lack of tracking for GDPR requests. If a candidate asked for their data to be deleted, the company might do it, but there was no reliable record showing who handled it, what was checked, and when it was closed.

### What would you improve first?

I would start with a GDPR request tracker. It is simple, low cost, and gives immediate visibility of open requests, owners, due dates, and evidence.

### Why not recommend a full compliance system?

For a small recruitment agency, that could be expensive and hard to maintain. A lightweight tracker and data register would be easier for the team to adopt.

### What did this project show about your BA skills?

It shows that I can look at a messy manual process, identify practical gaps, connect them to business risk, and suggest improvements that fit the company size.

## 60-Second Version

I created a GDPR gap analysis for a fictional small recruitment agency in Dublin. They handled candidate and client data through email, Excel, and shared folders, with no formal compliance system.

The main problem was that GDPR requests were handled manually, so the company could not easily prove what was received, who handled it, or whether it was closed properly.

I identified gaps around request tracking, consent evidence, data storage, retention, ownership, and audit visibility.

My recommendation was to keep it realistic: introduce a simple GDPR request tracker, create a basic data register, add consent fields to the candidate tracker, and review overdue or old records monthly.

The main business value was better control, less manual searching, fewer missed requests, and more trust with candidates.
